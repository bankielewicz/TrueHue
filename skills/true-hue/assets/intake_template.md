# Personal Color Analysis — Intake Protocol

Thank you for requesting a color analysis. To produce results comparable to a professional $300-$400 consultation, I need photos that meet a specific standard and written answers to the questionnaire below. **Shortcuts at the intake stage produce unreliable results** — please read the full protocol before taking photos.

---

## Privacy notes

Before you submit photos of your face, here's what's accurate about how they're handled:

- **Within this conversation:** The photos you submit are part of this chat session. When the conversation ends or is deleted, the photos are no longer retained in a way that persists to future sessions.
- **Not used for training:** Anthropic does not use chat content (including images) for training by default. You can verify current policy in Anthropic's published policies.
- **Deletion control:** You can delete this conversation any time from your chat history, which removes the photos from your account view. Retention in Anthropic's backend systems follows Anthropic's standard data retention schedule.
- **Screenshot and share considerations:** If you screenshot Claude's response (including any restated or described photo content) and share it elsewhere, those screenshots are outside Claude's and Anthropic's privacy controls.

**Minimizing identifying information:** If you prefer, you can crop photos tightly to show only the face/skin area needed, and avoid including identifying background, jewelry, clothing brand markers, or clearly visible name/ID tags. This does not reduce analysis quality.

**If you're uncomfortable submitting photos at all:** The analysis can still be attempted using only the questionnaire answers, with significant confidence reduction. Say so at the start and we can proceed accordingly. This is meaningfully less accurate than photo-based analysis.

## Part A: Photo requirements

### Lighting

- **Use natural daylight only**, indirect. Best options:
  - Near a north-facing window (no direct sunbeam)
  - Outdoors on an overcast day, in open shade
  - Midday (10am–2pm) but **never** direct sunlight on the face
- **Do NOT use**: indoor warm bulbs, fluorescent lights, flash, ring lights, or mixed lighting (e.g., daylight + lamp). These create color casts that contaminate the analysis.

### Skin and face preparation

- **No makeup.** None. Bare skin, freshly washed.
- **No filters, beauty mode, or auto-enhance.** Turn these off in your camera settings.
- **No color correction** in post. Raw photos only.
- **Hair pulled fully back** — off the face, ears, and neck. Use a headband or pull it into a low ponytail behind you. Colored hair visible in the frame will skew the reading.
- **Remove glasses** for the eye close-up (shot 3). For other shots, clear glasses are acceptable.

### Clothing

- Wear a **plain white or medium-gray** shirt, or drape a neutral towel/sheet over your shoulders covering your collarbone area.
- No colored clothing within six inches of your face. Colored fabric reflects onto the jaw and neck and changes the apparent skin tone.

### White balance reference (critical)

- Hold a sheet of **plain white printer paper** (standard copier paper, not cream or off-white) up next to your cheek in at least the first photo. This gives the analysis a known neutral to calibrate against.

### Device settings to check

- **iPhone**: Set Photographic Styles to "Standard." Turn off Portrait mode and Night mode.
- **Samsung/Android**: Turn off Scene Optimizer, Beauty, and any AI Photo enhancement.
- **Any phone**: Turn off filters, beauty mode, and flash. Use the rear camera if possible — front cameras are often lower quality and more aggressive with auto-correction.
- **Avoid laptop webcams** — image quality is usually insufficient for color analysis.

### Solo photographer solutions

The photo protocol requires holding white printer paper at your cheek while capturing front-of-face, side profile, and hand shots. Solo photographers can't do all of these single-handed. Options:

**Option 1: Tape paper to mirror, use rear camera.** Tape a sheet of white printer paper to a mirror at cheek height when you stand in front of it. Stand facing the mirror, use your phone's rear camera (better quality than front camera) held to your side or slightly below, and capture your reflection plus the paper. The paper's position relative to the face is consistent without requiring a hand to hold it.

**Option 2: Prop paper against an object.** Lean the paper against a lamp, a stack of books, or a small stand at chest height. Position yourself so the paper is at cheek level when you stand. Leaves both hands free for the camera.

**Option 3: Camera timer + phone stand.** Set your phone on a stable surface at face height (stack of books, actual tripod if you have one). Use the timer function (3-10 seconds). You have both hands free to hold the paper at cheek level. Works for all 5 required shots.

**Option 4: Ask a household member.** If someone is available, having them hold the phone while you hold the paper makes the protocol trivial. The assistant does not need any training — just has to hit the shutter button.

**What doesn't work:**
- Selfie sticks (introduce their own color cast and often have poor camera quality at the required distance)
- Propping the paper on your shoulder (shifts lighting; paper reflects onto shoulder instead of next to face)
- Holding the paper in your mouth (don't)
- Skipping the paper entirely (the white balance reference is non-optional — see the Lighting section above for why)

### If none of these are feasible

If mobility issues or environment constraints make all four options impossible, contact Claude during intake and describe the constraint. A degraded-mode analysis can still be run with clear confidence caveats. Honest constraints are workable; pretending the protocol was followed when it wasn't produces false-confidence results.

## Part B: Required photos (five minimum)

| # | Shot | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Front of face, neutral expression, white paper held at cheek level | Primary skin tone reading + white balance reference |
| 2 | Side profile of face (either side) | Jawline undertone, ear/neck skin reading |
| 3 | Close-up of eyes, both open, looking at camera (no glasses) | Iris color, flecks, limbal ring, depth |
| 4 | Inner wrist, palm-up, natural light | Vein color — undertone cross-check |
| 5 | Back of hand flat on a sheet of white printer paper | Skin-against-neutral comparison |
| 6 (optional) | Inner forearm, palm-up, on sheet of white paper | Less sun-exposed skin for undertone cross-check |

Optional but helpful:
- Full hair color in natural light (top-of-head shot)
- Any photo of you in a color that made you look *great* and one where you looked *washed out* (for reference)

### Shot 6 (optional but highly recommended): Inner forearm

The inner forearm gets less sun exposure than the face, which often makes undertone easier to read reliably. Place your inner forearm flat on a sheet of white printer paper in natural daylight and photograph it from above. Include the wrist and as much of the forearm as the frame allows.

This shot is especially valuable if:
- Your face has significant sun exposure or tanning
- You have rosacea, melasma, or other pigmentation changes concentrated on the face
- You have facial hair obscuring jawline skin
- Standard undertone reads differ between face and inner wrist (if you know this from prior analysis)

### Note on shot 4 for deeper skin tones

If you have deeply pigmented skin and veins aren't easily visible on your wrist, don't worry — take the photo anyway (the skin reading is still useful), and note in the questionnaire that your veins aren't visible. The analysis will rely on the fabric draping test (below) and other cross-checks instead. This is standard for deep-skin analysis and doesn't reduce the quality of the result.

### Optional but powerful: fabric draping test

The single most reliable undertone test is draping pure white fabric vs. cream/off-white fabric against your face in natural light. If you can, take two additional photos:

- **Shot 7 (optional):** Face with pure white fabric draped at the shoulders
- **Shot 8 (optional):** Face with cream/off-white fabric draped at the shoulders

Notice which one makes your face look more radiant vs. more drained. Report this observation in the questionnaire. This test is especially valuable for deep skin, where it's the most reliable undertone signal available.

## Part C: Questionnaire

Please answer all of these. Several of these attributes cannot be seen in photos.

1. **Natural hair color** — the color your hair grew in as (childhood or before any dye). Current hair: is it colored? What color is it now?

2. **Sun response** — when you're in the sun without sunscreen: do you burn, tan, both, or neither? How quickly? Do you freckle?

3. **Jewelry test** — against your bare skin (try on a clean wrist or neck), which looks more harmonious: yellow gold, silver/platinum, rose gold? Does one make your skin look tired or sallow? (Remember: we're asking which makes your skin *glow*, not which you prefer aesthetically.)

4. **Fabric draping test (if you did shots 7-8)** — which fabric made your face look more alive: pure white or cream/off-white? Or did both work about equally?

5. **Compliments** — which specific colors have strangers or acquaintances complimented you in? (Examples welcome: "that teal top," "the forest green sweater")

6. **Fails** — which colors make you look tired, washed out, grey, yellowish, or sick? Be specific.

7. **Foundation history (if applicable)** — what foundation shades have worked for you? What shades have turned orange, grey, or pink on you? This is a powerful undertone signal, especially if you have deeper skin.

8. **Eyes** — describe your eye color in detail. Does it shift in different lighting (e.g., "green in sunlight, grey indoors")? Any flecks, rings, or a darker rim around the iris?

9. **Wrist veins** — on the inside of your wrist in natural light, do your veins look blue, purple, green, or a mix? (If you can't see your veins easily, that's fine — just say so. This is common for deeper skin and doesn't affect the analysis.)

10. **Heritage** (optional) — ethnic or genetic background. Undertone patterns have loose correlations with heritage, but individual variation dominates. This helps as one weak input among many, nothing more.

11. **Dye history** — have you ever dyed your hair a color that looked *wrong* on you? What color and why did it look wrong?

12. **Current life factors** — are you currently pregnant, recently post-partum, going through menopause, on medication that affects skin color, or dealing with a significant sunburn or tan? (These can temporarily shift apparent undertone.)

12b. **Skin variation between areas** — Does your skin tone vary noticeably between sun-exposed areas (face, hands, forearms) and non-exposed areas (inner arm, stomach, upper thigh)? If yes, describe. This helps distinguish sun-adapted apparent coloring from baseline undertone.

12c. **Color vision** — Do you have any form of color vision deficiency? Options:
- No known color vision issues
- Deuteranopia / red-green deficiency (most common)
- Protanopia (red deficient)
- Tritanopia (blue-yellow deficiency, rare)
- Total color blindness (monochromacy, very rare)
- Not sure — but I sometimes confuse colors others see clearly

This does not affect your color analysis outcome. It affects how the deliverable is formatted so it's usable to you. If you answered anything except "No known," I'll include a color-blind-accessible format alongside the standard hex-code output.

13. **Context** — what's driving the analysis? Wardrobe planning, upcoming event, considering a hair color change, curiosity? This affects what I emphasize in the deliverable.

13a. **Profession and work environment** — What do you do, and what's the dress code? This affects which palette roles to emphasize in the deliverable. Examples:
- Corporate (suit and tie / business formal)
- Business casual (slacks and button-downs / blazers optional)
- Creative professional (more flexibility, more color expression)
- Remote or casual-only (athleisure, jeans, casual tops)
- Uniform-based (medical scrubs, service industry uniform, etc. — note that baseline uniform color may not be in your palette and that's OK)
- Not currently employed / retired / other

13b. **Climate** — Where do you live, and what's the general temperature range? Examples:
- Hot / tropical (rarely below 60°F / 15°C, much of the year above 80°F / 27°C)
- Hot summer / mild winter (most of US South, coastal regions)
- Four-season with mild winter (most temperate regions)
- Cold winter / warm summer (Northeast US, Northern Europe, similar)
- Cold / harsh winter (Northern tier, Nordic countries, similar)

This affects fiber recommendations and which subset of your palette gets emphasized in the Starter Capsule.

13c. **Formal-to-casual ratio** — Out of a typical week, what percentage of your time is spent in each category?
- Formal (suits, dresses, professional attire)
- Business casual
- Everyday casual (jeans, sweaters, basics)
- Athletic / loungewear

Rough percentage estimate is fine. This affects which wardrobe pieces the Starter Capsule prioritizes.

14. **Grooming categories** — check all that apply (I'll tailor the deliverable accordingly):
    - [ ] Skincare and tinted products (tinted moisturizer, tinted SPF, lip balm with color)
    - [ ] Full makeup (foundation, eyeshadow, lipstick, blush)
    - [ ] Facial hair (beard, mustache, goatee)
    - [ ] Eyewear (glasses, sunglasses — I can recommend frame colors)
    - [ ] Jewelry (I'll always cover this)
    - [ ] Hair color (if you dye or are considering it)
    - [ ] Ties and pocket squares
    - [ ] Watches and other accessories

    Nothing here is gender-specific. Pick what's relevant to your actual grooming practice.

## What happens next

Once you submit, the analysis runs in these phases:

1. **Photo quality gate** — I'll verify your photos meet the protocol. If any fail, I'll tell you specifically what to re-shoot.
2. **Dimension analysis** — I'll work through undertone, value, chroma, and contrast separately, citing evidence from your photos and answers.
3. **Preliminary check-in** — I'll share my reading of your four dimensions and ask if anything feels off before proceeding. This catches errors when they're cheap to fix.
4. **Season determination with draping comparison** — I'll determine your season and explicitly justify why not the two adjacent seasons.
5. **Full deliverable** — a complete palette with 30-40 hex codes, colors to avoid, metals, grooming guidance (based on your selected categories), neutrals, starter capsule, and confidence notes.

Optionally, I can also generate PDF swatch cards (full-page, wallet-sized, or phone wallpaper) for portable shopping reference.

If anything about the protocol is unclear or infeasible for you (access to natural light, mobility constraints, etc.), tell me — I can work with you on alternatives with appropriate caveats.
