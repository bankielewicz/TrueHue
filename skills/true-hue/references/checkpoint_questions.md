# Phase 3.5 Checkpoint Question Library

Vetted questions Claude draws from when running the mandatory Phase 3.5 preliminary check-in. These questions surface disagreement, not confirmation — their purpose is to catch dimension-reading errors while corrections are still cheap.

### How the check-in works

Phase 3.5 runs after the four dimensions are read but before a season is named. Claude presents the readings with evidence, then asks a small number of targeted questions drawn from this library. If the user pushes back with new evidence, Claude adjusts before moving to Phase 4. If the user confirms or offers only preference-based pushback, Claude holds the reading and proceeds.

**Core principle — ask questions whose answers could change your mind.** "Do you agree you're muted?" is useless; the user will say yes whether or not they are. "Do you feel most alive in complex layered colors or in clear decisive ones?" forces a choice that actually discriminates muted vs. bright.

**When to use dimension-specific vs. cross-cutting questions.** Dimension-specific questions target the reading you feel least confident about. Cross-cutting questions work when multiple dimensions are simultaneously uncertain or when the user's evidence pattern seems internally contradictory. Typical check-in = 1 dimension question + 1 cross-check. High-uncertainty cases = up to 3 questions.

### Undertone questions

1. **Jewelry in natural light.** "Put on your favorite gold piece, then your favorite silver piece, in natural daylight, and look at the skin immediately next to the metal — not the metal itself. Which one makes the skin look more alive versus which one makes it look duller or greyer? (If you can only check one kind because you only own one, tell me which.)"
   - *Tests:* Undertone. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User reports the opposite metal harmonizes than the one your reading predicted.
   - *When to use:* Any skin depth — jewelry test is reliable across the board.

2. **Compliment pattern check.** "When you remember the last 4–5 times someone complimented what you were wearing — not the style, the color — what were those colors? Not 'I think I look good in' but 'someone else said something.'"
   - *Tests:* Undertone, often chroma too. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* Compliments cluster warm when you read cool, or cluster vivid when you read muted.
   - *When to use:* Always useful. Particularly valuable when users have been overly influenced by personal aesthetic preference.

3. **Foundation behavior history.** "What's happened to foundations you've tried? Specifically: have any turned orange on you after a few hours, or looked grey or ashy right after application, or looked fine in the store and wrong at home? Brand and shade names if you remember them."
   - *Tests:* Undertone. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* Foundations oxidizing suggests user's undertone is warmer than the product's base, often indicating the user is warmer than you read; foundations going grey can signal an olive undertone you missed.
   - *When to use:* Especially valuable for deep skin (where vein test is unreliable) and for anyone with extensive makeup history. Skip for users who don't wear foundation.

4. **White vs. cream fabric drape.** "If you can, grab a pure white T-shirt or printer paper and a cream/off-white or ivory one. Hold each up to your face in natural light. Which makes your face look lit up from inside versus which makes you look tired or washed out? If you haven't done this, can you try it in the next day and come back?"
   - *Tests:* Undertone (most reliable DIY test). *Suggests reading is wrong if:* Cream flatters a user you read as cool, or pure white flatters a user you read as warm.
   - *When to use:* Gold standard for deep skin. Also valuable whenever undertone confidence is moderate or lower.

5. **Invisible color.** "What's a color you've put on — a sweater, a top, a dress — where you caught yourself in a mirror and thought 'I disappear in this' or 'I look washed out'? Not a color you dislike aesthetically, a color where you noticed your face got quieter."
   - *Tests:* Undertone and often chroma. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User's invisible colors are warm (suggesting they're actually cool) or muted (suggesting they're actually bright) opposite to your reading.
   - *When to use:* Great general-purpose question; particularly useful when compliment memory is weak but fail memory is strong.

### Value questions

1. **Very light color comfort.** "When you wear something very light — pale pink, soft cream, light sky blue — does your face read crisp and lifted, or does it start to look tired and fade into the light color?"
   - *Tests:* Value. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User reads light-value (light colors should flatter) but reports consistent fading in lights.
   - *When to use:* When value reading straddles light and medium.

2. **Very dark color comfort.** "When you wear pure black or very deep colors (chocolate, deep navy, forest), does your face stand out against the darkness, or does it look small and overwhelmed by the piece?"
   - *Tests:* Value. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User reads deep-value but reports overwhelm from darks, or vice versa.
   - *When to use:* When distinguishing medium-deep from deep, or deciding whether user can handle Winter's pure black.

3. **Feature contrast observation.** "Look at yourself in a mirror. Place your hand over your hair and look at just your skin and eyes — how different are the values? Then block your eyes and look at your skin and hair — are those similar or very different? Is any pair (skin/hair, skin/eyes, hair/eyes) dramatically different or dramatically similar?"
   - *Tests:* Value, feeds directly into Contrast. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User's self-report of feature spread contradicts photo read.
   - *When to use:* When value and contrast are interacting confusingly.

4. **Mid-tone read.** "When you wear medium-depth colors — like medium gray, medium denim, camel — do you feel like they sit comfortably with your coloring, or does your face compete with them for attention?"
   - *Tests:* Value (are mids the user's home territory). *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User reads medium-value but mid-tones consistently fight their face.
   - *When to use:* When value reading is medium but chroma is uncertain.

5. **Deepest/lightest hair.** "What's the darkest your natural hair has ever been — baby hair, childhood, adolescence, now? And the lightest (summer sun, childhood blonde, any natural variation)? The range across your life can tell us things your current hair can't."
   - *Tests:* Value (natural coloring including points of history). *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User's natural-hair range is markedly different from current; especially valuable for users who've dyed extensively.
   - *When to use:* When current hair is dyed or natural range is unclear.

### Chroma questions

1. **Muted vs. vivid preference.** "Two outfits, same style: one in richly saturated colors (clear red, bright teal, pure yellow), the other in complex softer colors (rust, muted sage, dusty mustard). Not which you'd pick by style — which makes you feel more like yourself?"
   - *Tests:* Chroma. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User reads muted but consistently feels most alive in clear saturation.
   - *When to use:* Core chroma question, usable almost anywhere.

2. **Jewel tones vs. dusty tones.** "Think of jewel tones (emerald, sapphire, amethyst — rich and clear) versus dusty tones (sage, dusty rose, muted plum — softened). Which group do you tend to reach for, and more importantly, which group gets you compliments?"
   - *Tests:* Chroma. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* Compliments cluster in the opposite group from your reading.
   - *When to use:* When chroma is the least-confident dimension.

3. **Decisive vs. complex colors.** "When you shop, do you find yourself drawn to colors that are clearly one thing — clear red, clear blue — or to colors that feel layered, where you couldn't quite name them (is it grey? brown? purple? something in between)?"
   - *Tests:* Chroma (preference) — follow-up separates preference from suitability. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User's consistent gravitation contradicts your reading AND their compliment history supports their pull.
   - *When to use:* For aesthetic-preference users who haven't clearly separated preference from what works.

4. **Recent compliment clarity.** "Of your last few color compliments, try to recall: were those colors clear (clear-cut and crisp when you described them) or complex (you had to think a moment about what color exactly it was)?"
   - *Tests:* Chroma. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User's compliment colors cluster on the opposite clarity from your reading.
   - *When to use:* Whenever compliment memory is available but hasn't clarified chroma.

5. **What you avoid in your own wardrobe.** "What colors are you essentially never drawn to — not 'ugly' in some objective sense, but 'never has a place in my wardrobe'? Cataloging what you've consistently avoided over the years is often a strong signal."
   - *Tests:* Chroma and sometimes undertone. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User consistently avoids muted-family colors (suggests they're bright) or avoids clear-saturated colors (suggests they're muted).
   - *When to use:* When preference-based patterns need to be surfaced.

### Contrast questions

1. **High-contrast outfit comfort.** "When you wear an outfit with strong contrast — black + white, deep navy + pure cream — does the outfit frame your face or overpower it? Does it feel like you're wearing it, or like it's wearing you?"
   - *Tests:* Contrast. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* Low-contrast reading but user comfortable in high-contrast combos, or high-contrast reading but overwhelmed by them.
   - *When to use:* Central contrast question.

2. **Feature value spread.** "Looking at your unfiltered reflection: if you had to say 'my features are all in similar value territory' or 'there's a big jump between my darkest feature and my lightest,' which is closer to true?"
   - *Tests:* Contrast directly. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* Self-description contradicts photo read.
   - *When to use:* When contrast read seems underspecified.

3. **Monochrome comfort.** "How do you feel in head-to-toe outfits where everything is in the same value range — all beiges, all soft greys, all dusty pastels? Does it read as polished on you, or does your face get lost in it?"
   - *Tests:* Contrast (low-contrast tolerance). *Suggests reading is wrong if:* High-contrast reading but user polished in monochrome, or low-contrast reading but lost in it.
   - *When to use:* Distinguishing low from medium contrast.

4. **Face-framing color boldness.** "At the face — scarf, collar, earrings, necklace — can you wear something bold (a saturated color, a high-contrast print) without it overtaking you, or do you look best with softer, closer-to-skin choices near the face?"
   - *Tests:* Contrast (especially for high-contrast seasons). *Suggests reading is wrong if:* User tolerates bold face-framing beyond what your contrast reading allows.
   - *When to use:* When deciding between contrast levels and whether the user can handle Winter-level brightness near the face.

5. **The 'washed out' moment.** "What's a specific time you've felt washed out — maybe in a work photo, on a bad-skin day, in a particular light? Not 'I was tired,' but 'the color I was wearing wasn't holding me up.' What color were you wearing?"
   - *Tests:* Contrast and often value. *Suggests reading is wrong if:* The washing-out color is inside the palette you're about to give them.
   - *When to use:* When past-experience data helps anchor the reading in lived reality.

### Cross-cutting questions

Use these when multiple dimensions are simultaneously uncertain, or when the user's evidence pattern seems internally contradictory.

1. **Three compliment outfits — what's the pattern.** "Think of three specific outfits you've been complimented on more than once. Not just that people said 'nice shirt,' but 'that color.' Describe all three. I'll look for the pattern across them."
   - *Tests:* All four dimensions simultaneously. *Use when:* The dimension readings don't feel cohesive.

2. **Three wrong outfits — what's the pattern.** "Think of three outfits that felt off — you looked in the mirror and something wasn't working, or a photo came back and you looked tired. Describe all three. The pattern across fails is often clearer than the pattern across wins."
   - *Tests:* All four dimensions by negative example. *Use when:* Compliment memory is weak but fail memory is strong.

3. **Comfort zone vs. aspirational zone.** "What colors do you currently own and wear comfortably? And what colors do you wish you could wear but don't reach for? Sometimes the aspirational zone points at a bad read — either the user's season really is where their comfort already is, or the aspirational colors are actually on-palette and the user's been gun-shy."
   - *Tests:* Often undertone and chroma. *Use when:* User has expressed preference-based pushback during the check-in and you want to separate preference from evidence.

### Selection guidance

**Typical check-in (2 questions):**
- Pick 1 question from the dimension you feel least confident about
- Pick 1 cross-check — either from another dimension that feeds into the same season decision, or one of the cross-cutting three

**High-uncertainty case (up to 3 questions):**
- 1 per most-uncertain dimension, plus 1 cross-cutting
- Do not exceed 3 — more than that turns the check-in into an interrogation and loses the user

**For deep-skin users:**
- Prefer foundation behavior and compliment pattern questions over vein- or sun-response-based logic
- Favor the white vs. cream fabric drape question if they haven't already done the test

**For fair-to-medium skin:**
- Jewelry and wrist-vein-related questions carry more weight
- Compliment pattern remains universal

**When the check-in is skippable:**
- It isn't. Phase 3.5 is mandatory regardless of confidence. Even a textbook-clean reading goes through the check-in — some of the biggest misses happen on cases that looked obvious.
