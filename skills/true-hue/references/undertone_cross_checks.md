# Undertone Cross-Checks — Including Deep-Skin Protocol

The standard undertone tests (vein color, gold-vs-silver jewelry, sun response) were developed on predominantly light-to-medium skin. Their reliability drops significantly on deeper skin. This reference documents when each test works, when it doesn't, and what to use instead.

Claude should consult this reference during Phase 3 (Dimension 1: Undertone), especially when working with deep-skin users.

---

## Test reliability by skin depth

| Test | Fair/Light | Medium | Medium-Deep | Deep/Very Deep |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vein color | Reliable | Mostly reliable | Decreasingly reliable | **Unreliable — skip** |
| Gold vs. silver jewelry | Reliable | Reliable | Reliable | Reliable |
| Sun response (burn/tan) | Reliable | Reliable | Limited discrimination | Limited discrimination |
| White vs. cream fabric | Reliable | Reliable | **Most reliable** | **Most reliable** |
| Jawline vs. white paper | Reliable | Reliable | Reliable | Reliable |
| Compliment/fail memory | Reliable across all depths | — | — | — |
| Foundation jaw-to-chest | — | Reliable | **Highly reliable** | **Highly reliable** |

**Key insight:** For deep skin, the most reliable tests are fabric draping (white vs. cream), foundation matching behavior, and compliment/fail recall. The least reliable are vein color and sun response.

## The tests explained

### Vein color test

- **How it works**: Look at veins on the inner wrist in natural daylight. Blue/purple veins suggest cool undertone; green veins suggest warm; mixed suggests neutral.
- **Why it fails on deep skin**: higher melanin concentration reduces the visual contrast between veins and surrounding skin. On very deep skin, veins may not be visible at all, or may appear gray/neutral regardless of true undertone.
- **When to use**: confident readings on fair-to-medium skin only. On deep skin, note "vein test inconclusive due to natural melanin depth" and move on.

### Gold vs. silver jewelry test

- **How it works**: Place gold jewelry against bare skin, then silver. Warm undertones harmonize with gold; cool with silver; neutral with both.
- **Why it works across skin depths**: the test is about harmony/dissonance, not absolute color visibility. A deep-skin person can still observe whether gold pulls out golden highlights in their complexion or whether silver makes them look grey.
- **Common trap**: personal aesthetic preference. Someone may prefer silver but actually look better in gold. The test is about which makes the skin *glow* vs. *dull*, not which looks like nicer jewelry.
- **Best practice**: have the user try both in natural daylight, look at the skin immediately adjacent to the metal, and report which makes the skin look more alive.

### Sun response test

- **How it works**: Cool-undertone people tend to burn without tanning; warm-undertone people tend to tan easily. Neutral undertones often do both.
- **Why it's limited for deep skin**: high-melanin skin doesn't burn in the same way — it's already protected. Most deep-skin people tan further or stay stable, which doesn't discriminate warm from cool.
- **When to use**: supporting signal for fair-to-medium skin only.

### White vs. cream fabric test (MOST RELIABLE FOR DEEP SKIN)

- **How it works**: Hold a piece of pure white fabric next to the face, then a piece of cream/off-white. One will make the skin look radiant; the other will look draining or off.
  - If pure white flatters → cool undertone
  - If cream flatters → warm undertone
  - If both work → neutral
- **Why it's reliable across all skin depths**: this test measures skin's response to a specific light reflection, not the absolute color of the skin itself. It works regardless of base skin depth.
- **Professional standard**: this is what professional color analysts use for draping. It's the closest DIY equivalent to a $400 consultation.
- **Setup**: user needs both fabrics (or papers), natural daylight, no makeup, hair pulled back. Takes about 60 seconds per fabric.

### Jawline against white paper (visual reading)

- **How it works**: In photo 1, the white paper is held at the jawline. Examine the skin's cast against the neutral paper.
  - Golden/yellow/peachy cast → warm
  - Pink/rosy/bluish cast → cool
  - Olive/greenish/balanced → neutral or olive
- **Why it works across skin depths**: the paper is a fixed reference. All skin tones show a discernible undertone cast against pure white if the lighting is clean.
- **Caveat**: photography variance. If the paper itself reads off-white in the photo, the reading is unreliable. Always check paper neutrality first.

### Foundation jaw-to-chest behavior

- **How it works**: When someone with the right undertone applies foundation and blends from jaw to chest, it disappears seamlessly. Wrong undertone creates visible discontinuity:
  - Foundation turns orange or oxidizes → base is too warm for the skin
  - Foundation looks grey or ashy → base is too cool for the skin, OR too warm (on olive-deep skin specifically)
  - Foundation looks pink → base is too cool
- **Why it's powerful for deep skin**: this is the behavior professional makeup artists use to match. Deep-skin users often have extensive lived experience with foundation failures — asking them which brands/shades have and haven't worked is a rich data source.
- **In the questionnaire**: the "fail colors" and "foundation history" answers carry the most weight for deep-skin undertone determination.

### Compliment and fail memory

- **How it works**: the user's lived experience of compliments and bad outfits is data. Patterns cluster by undertone.
  - Repeatedly complimented in olive, rust, warm cream, mustard → likely warm
  - Repeatedly complimented in pure white, cool pink, royal blue, burgundy → likely cool
  - No strong pattern; mixed compliments → likely neutral
- **Why it works across all skin depths**: lived feedback doesn't depend on visual measurement — it depends on aggregated human response over years.
- **Limitation**: the user must be able to recall specifics. "I look good in everything" is not data. "I got three compliments on my olive green jumpsuit, and my pink blouse makes me feel invisible" is data.

## Specific protocol for deep skin

When skin is deeply pigmented (Fitzpatrick V-VI, or any skin where vein color is not easily visible), use this ordered approach:

1. **Start with fabric draping test (white vs. cream)** — this is your primary signal
2. **Cross-check with gold vs. silver jewelry** — secondary primary signal
3. **Weight the questionnaire heavily**:
   - Foundation behavior history ("does your foundation turn orange / grey / stay true?")
   - Compliment/fail color memory
   - Jewelry aesthetic preference (as one input among many)
4. **Jawline-against-white-paper** from photo 1 — reference cast
5. **Supporting: knuckle and palm-side fingertip tone** — often shows the clearest undertone
6. **Skip the vein test** or note it as "inconclusive" — do not force a reading
7. **Skip or de-weight sun response test** — limited discrimination

Document which signals you used. Example phrasing for the deliverable:

> "I determined your warm undertone through the white vs. cream fabric test (cream clearly flattered while pure white drained you), confirmed by gold jewelry harmonizing better than silver, and your foundation history (your oxidation complaints are classic for warm undertone placed in a too-warm base). The vein test was inconclusive due to natural melanin depth, which is expected — the other signals converged cleanly."

## Olive complexions (all skin depths)

Olive is an undertone category often missed because it doesn't fit the warm/cool binary cleanly. Olive skin has a green cast on top of an underlying warm, cool, or neutral base.

### Identifying olive

- Skin appears to have a slight green or grey-green cast, especially in certain lights
- Gold jewelry looks fine but silver doesn't look wrong either — both can work with nuance
- Against pure white, skin reads neither distinctly warm nor distinctly cool — there's a grey-green presence
- Foundation that's "warm enough" in depth still sometimes looks too yellow or too pink — neither base matches
- Often clusters in Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, South Asian, Southeast Asian, East African, Latin American, and some Eastern European heritage — but can occur anywhere

### Olive and the seasons

Olive complexions most commonly map to:
- **Soft Summer** (olive with cool-leaning base)
- **Soft Autumn** (olive with warm-leaning base)
- **Deep Autumn** (olive with warm-leaning base, deeper value)
- **Deep Winter** (olive with cool-neutral base, deeper value)

Less common but possible:
- Dark Winter (olive with cool base and high contrast)
- True Summer (olive with clearly cool base)

### Palette modifications for olive complexions

Olive-complexion users in any season should:
- Prefer slightly warmer versions of cool palettes, and slightly cooler versions of warm palettes
- Favor greens that *match* their own olive cast (harmonizes) rather than bright clear greens (clashes)
- Avoid pure pinks and pure yellows — both tend to emphasize the green cast
- Favor colors with similar complexity — muted, layered, slightly grey-down colors often work better than pure jewel tones

## Genetic/heritage clustering (handle with care)

Undertone patterns correlate loosely with some genetic heritage, but correlations are weak and individual variation dominates. Use heritage as a very weak prior — never as a determinant.

**Rough statistical tendencies** (not rules):
- Northern European heritage skews toward cool and warm-neutral, with less olive
- Mediterranean / South European heritage skews toward warm and olive
- Middle Eastern / North African heritage skews toward warm, warm-neutral, and olive
- South Asian heritage skews toward warm and warm-neutral, with significant olive
- East Asian heritage skews toward neutral and warm-neutral, with some olive
- Southeast Asian heritage skews toward warm and olive
- West African and Afro-descendant heritage shows the full spectrum — cool, warm, and neutral are all common; olive also occurs; generalizing here is particularly error-prone
- Indigenous American heritage skews toward warm and warm-neutral, with olive common
- Mixed heritage: anything is possible, and olive occurs frequently

**Do not** say: "Because you're [heritage], you're probably [undertone]."

**Do** say: "Heritage is one weak input among many. Your [specific test results] are stronger signals than heritage-based generalization."

## When undertone is genuinely ambiguous

Some people truly read as neutral — neither clearly warm nor clearly cool. This is a legitimate outcome, not a failed analysis.

Signs of genuine neutrality:
- White and cream fabrics both work (or neither clearly wins)
- Gold and silver both look fine
- Compliment history has both warm and cool colors
- Vein test (if visible) shows mixed blue-green

Neutral-undertone users typically land in border sub-seasons:
- Soft Summer (neutral leaning cool)
- Soft Autumn (neutral leaning warm)
- Light Summer (neutral leaning cool, light value)
- Light Spring (neutral leaning warm, light value)
- Deep Winter (neutral leaning cool, deep value)
- Deep Autumn (neutral leaning warm, deep value)

State this clearly in the deliverable rather than forcing a false binary: "Your undertone reads as genuinely neutral with a slight warm lean. You can comfortably wear colors from both Soft Autumn and Soft Summer palettes, with Soft Autumn being your better primary."

## Summary for Claude

When assessing undertone:
1. Identify the user's approximate skin depth from photos 1 and 5 first
2. For deep skin (Fitzpatrick V-VI), use the fabric draping + jewelry + questionnaire cluster, not the vein test
3. For olive complexions, acknowledge the complexity explicitly rather than forcing warm/cool
4. Cite which tests converged and which didn't
5. For genuinely neutral results, state that clearly and give border-season guidance
6. Never assume undertone from heritage alone

## Reading face vs. body coloring

The inner forearm or inner upper arm is a more reliable undertone reference than the face for users who:
- Have significant sun exposure on the face
- Have rosacea, melasma, acne, or other conditions affecting face skin
- Have visible pigmentation differences between face and body (common with heritage that tans differently than the baseline tone)

### When face and body readings differ

If the face reads warm but the inner arm reads neutral-cool (or vice versa), the inner arm is closer to the true undertone. The face has been altered by sun exposure or skin conditions.

**Protocol for discrepancy:**
1. Note the discrepancy explicitly in Phase 3 Dimension 1 output
2. Use the inner arm as the primary undertone reference
3. In the deliverable Confidence section, mention that face coloring and body coloring differ, and recommend sun protection / skincare adjustments if relevant to the user's goals (keep this brief — not medical advice)
4. If the discrepancy is extreme (e.g., heavily sun-damaged face + pale inner arm), confidence may need to drop to Moderate until the user can submit a re-shoot with reduced sun exposure (not always feasible)

### When only face photos were submitted

If the user skipped the optional Shot 6, proceed with face-only reading but note in the confidence section that body-skin cross-check was not performed. If undertone reading is already ambiguous from face photos alone, explicitly invite the user to submit the inner arm photo as a disambiguator.
