# Getting Started — Your First Session

This is an annotated walkthrough of what actually happens when you run a color analysis with True Hue for the first time. If you want the short version, the [README](../README.md) has it. This page is for when you want to know what to expect turn by turn before committing to the 20–30 minutes.

## Before you open Claude

Gather these ahead of time. The protocol goes smoothest when you're not scrambling for printer paper halfway through.

- Plain white printer paper. Not cream. Not the nice eggshell stuff. The kind that jams in your office printer.
- A plain white, medium-gray, or neutral top. Or a towel/sheet to drape at the shoulders.
- Makeup remover if you're wearing any. The protocol wants bare skin.
- A window with indirect natural daylight. North-facing windows are gold. An overcast day outdoors works. A bright lamp is not the same thing and will produce bad results.
- Your phone.
- About 20–30 minutes of uninterrupted time.

If you have a friend, spouse, or family member available to hit the shutter while you hold the paper, recruit them. It's not required, but it makes the photo session five minutes instead of fifteen.

## Turn 1 — You open the conversation

You can say anything that signals what you want. All of these work:

- "Can you walk me through a full color analysis?"
- "I'd like to figure out my color season."
- "What's my seasonal palette?"
- "Help me find colors that flatter me."

The skill activates automatically.

## Turn 2 — Phase 0 setup questions

Claude doesn't jump straight into the photo protocol. First it asks a few routing questions:

1. Have you been professionally color-typed before? If yes, what season?
2. What's driving this conversation — first-time discovery, verifying a prior result, evaluating items you own, or refining an existing palette?
3. How much time do you have?

Why this exists: a lot of people arrive thinking they want Mode 1 when they actually want Mode 3 (a quick single-item check) or Mode 4 (refining a palette they already have). Phase 0 saves you from a 25-minute intake when all you needed was a five-minute answer.

Answer honestly. If you've taken a quiz app and gotten a result you don't trust, say so — that's useful context.

## Turn 3 — You get the intake protocol

If Phase 0 landed on Mode 1 (full analysis), Claude sends you the intake template. It covers three things:

- **Part A: Photo requirements.** Lighting, device settings, skin/face prep, clothing, the white paper reference, and the solo-photographer workarounds if you're shooting yourself.
- **Part B: The five photos.** Front face, side profile, eyes close-up, inner wrist, hand on white paper. Optional sixth photo of the inner forearm (highly recommended). Optional shots 7 and 8 for fabric draping.
- **Part C: Questionnaire.** 15+ items: natural hair, sun response, jewelry harmony, compliments, fails, foundation history, eyes, veins, heritage, dye history, life factors, skin variation, color vision, profession, climate, formal-to-casual ratio, grooming categories.

Read it. Take your time. The quality of what you submit directly determines the quality of what you get back.

## Turn 4 — You take the photos and fill out the questionnaire

Budget 10–15 minutes for this. It's the longest part.

A few practical things to know:

- Take the photos all at once, in the same lighting session. Don't spread them across three days.
- Check each photo full-screen before moving on. Phone preview thumbnails lie about lighting issues.
- The white paper in photo 1 should look neutral white in the photo — not yellow, not blue, not pink. If it reads off-white, your ambient light is contaminated; move to a different window.
- If your veins aren't visible on the inner wrist (common with deep skin), don't force it. Note "veins not visible" in the questionnaire and move on. It doesn't hurt the analysis.
- Answer the questionnaire with specifics, not generalities. "I got complimented on my olive green sweater twice last month" beats "I look good in green sometimes."

Send everything in one message when you're ready.

## Turn 5 — Phase 2 quality gate

Claude inspects your photos against the protocol. One of four things happens:

- **Pass.** All photos clean, questionnaire complete. Claude proceeds to analysis.
- **Partial acceptance.** Four photos pass, one fails. Claude asks you to reshoot just the one. You don't redo the whole session.
- **Full reject.** Multiple issues. Claude tells you specifically what to fix and asks for a resubmission.
- **Degraded mode.** If you can't reshoot and the photos are marginal, Claude offers to proceed with explicit confidence reduction. Moderate confidence is legitimate; fake confidence isn't.

Expect to reshoot something on your first attempt. Don't take it personally — the quality gate is strict because the cheap tools aren't, and their results show.

## Turn 6 — Phase 3 four-dimension analysis

Claude works through four dimensions separately, citing evidence from specific photos and questionnaire items:

- **Undertone** (warm / cool / neutral / olive) — from skin cast, wrist veins, jewelry test, foundation history, fabric drape, compliment pattern
- **Value** (light / medium / deep) — from hair, skin, and eye value
- **Chroma** (bright / muted / between) — from eye clarity, hair and skin quality
- **Contrast** (high / medium / low) — from feature value spread

This is not "you look like a Winter." It's "green wrist veins visible in photo 4, gold jewelry harmony reported, golden cast along jawline in photo 2, compliment cluster in olive/rust/warm-cream — converges on warm undertone with high confidence."

## Turn 7 — Phase 3.5 check-in (the most important turn)

Before naming a season, Claude presents the four dimension readings and asks you two targeted questions — drawn from a library designed specifically to surface misreads, not confirm agreement.

**Read Claude's reading carefully.** This is where errors get caught cheaply. If something feels off — either the reading or the evidence Claude cited — say so specifically.

Evidence-based pushback works: "Actually, the rust sweater I said worked? It's more of a burnt orange with a lot of brown in it, not a clear rust." Claude will adjust.

Preference-based pushback ("I want to be a Spring") doesn't. Claude will hold the analytical line and explain why. That's a feature. Color analysis tells you what flatters, not what you prefer.

This step is mandatory. Even on textbook-clean readings. It's not optional, and it's not skippable.

## Turn 8 — Phase 4 draping comparison

Claude names your season and explicitly addresses the two adjacent seasons on the flow chart — why not *this* neighbor, why not *that* neighbor. This is the text equivalent of a professional analyst physically draping three fabric swatches against your face.

If the evidence is genuinely balanced between two adjacent seasons, Claude says so and offers a between-seasons workflow rather than forcing a false binary. This is rare but it happens, and it's a legitimate outcome.

## Turn 9 — Phase 5 full deliverable

Now you get the palette. Full structure:

1. Analysis summary (dimensions with evidence)
2. Draping comparison (why this season)
3. Core palette (~36 hex codes by role)
4. Color pairing rules within your palette
5. Fabric and sheen guidance tied to your chroma and value
6. Colors to avoid (8–12 with reasons)
7. Metals
8. Your neutrals specifically
9. Face-framing vs. distance rules
10. Pattern and print recommendations
11. Conditional grooming sections (only the categories you selected)
12. Starter capsule at budget and investment tiers
13. Confidence and caveats

Expect this to be a long message. It's the deliverable you paid for in time and photos. Save it.

## Turn 10 — Phase 6 PDF offer

Claude offers to generate one or more PDF formats for portable reference:

- Full-page reference PDF (printable, comprehensive)
- Wallet card PDF (pocketable, 12 colors)
- Phone wallpaper PNG (lock-screen-able)

Opt in for whichever ones you want. The full page is useful for home reference; the wallet card is the winner for shopping trips; the phone wallpaper is good if you shop online and want glanceable access.

## Turn 11 — Phase 7 walkthrough

Finally, Claude offers to walk through any section in more detail — questions about specific colors, why you're your season versus a neighbor, specific grooming categories, starter capsule prioritization, practical shopping tips. Ask whatever you want. This is the part that mimics what you'd pay a consultant for in follow-up time.

When you've got what you need, you can save the conversation and come back later if new questions arise. If you wear pieces from the palette and something isn't working, there's a post-delivery validation workflow — just tell Claude what happened and which colors failed.

## What happens after

The palette is a starting point, not a prescription. Test it against lived experience over a few weeks of wear. If patterns feel off, come back for validation — the post-delivery workflow handles adjustments, anything from a small hex-code substitution within the season to a reconsideration if the pattern suggests the reading was off.

And if you're making a high-stakes decision where accuracy matters more than cost — a real analyst for an hour is still worth the money. This plugin is honest about its limits.
